Learning [[LANGUAGE]] - Introduction

by Gary Shannon

What this is all about

This web page is the framework, or template for a textbook for learning a language, any language. You can use this template to write you own textbook to teach any language you like, including your own invented languages. Each lesson has paragraphs marked with red shading that tell you what that paragraph needs to teach about your language.

Then when you have written your own explanation of the topic being discussed you can remove the red shaded text leaving only your own language lesson text.

Following each explanation section is the exercises section. There are three kinds of exercises: 1) Translation from your language to English; 2) Translation from English to your language; and 3) Answering questions in your language. The exercises are meant to reinforce the grammatical principles, and to use the vocabulary presented earlier in the lesson.

Wherever you, as the author of your very own language textbook, are to insert your own translations, you will see the text enclosed in [[double square brackets]]. So, for example, An exercise might have the sentence: "[[I have a pencil.]]" with the answer given as "I have a pencil." This means that you, as the textbook author, must translate the first sentence, replacing "[[I have a pencil]]" with "Me got scribbler." or whatever "I have a pencil" is in your language. That way, the student reading your textbook will have to translate it back into English, and then check his answer, which will be "I have a pencil."

Every question includes the answer, hidden from view until the question is clicked. Here are a few samples to show you how it works. These samples are asking for you to translate each sentence.

Good morning. (Click on the sentence to see the answer.)
[[Good morning.]] (Click above to hide the answer once more.)
See you later.
Chao, baby.
Do you speak English? (Anything special about this sentence? Put it here.)
Speeken ze Inglish?

By the way, you can replace this index.html page with your own page explaining your phonology, or whatever other introductory remarks you want to have before starting lesson 1.

There are exactly 50 lessons, covering every grammatical topic necessary to read and write sentences of arbitrary complexity in any tense and mood. The vocabulary provided with each lesson brings the total lexicon to roughly 700 words.
