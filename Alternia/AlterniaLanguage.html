If one desires to go to Alternia, you can do worse than learn the local language. Trolls are of course extremely territorial and very willing to squash any resemblance of outsided-ness, so it would be most optimal to at least know some parts of the language. This article describes the Reference Alternian Language, which of course is the imperial standard.

The Speaking Environment

The fact that every Troll speaks Alternian does not imply that Trolls have a unified language. Even as the Trolls are fiercely adding new planets and other spoils of war to their coffers, they started off much like humans did, with their own differences that evolve as life would: through the mechanisms of selection. To our end, this means that although the government tends to ignore the fact that Trolls have many languages, those languages do exist, even if only as a matrix to the RAL core.

Throughout the description of the language, one would notice that there are many regional variations that are due to the underlying language, that is, the language which RAL attempts to take over. A lot of strange variations are explained by the concept of such an underlying language, even though in a large number of cases these languages are lost, deliberately or accidentally. There are of course, places that don't have an underlying language – most notably, the RAL perfectly fits the language spoken inside the seat of government (which is not very surprising at all), so the locations around the seat of government don't have much of an underlying language to speak of.

Relations With Other Languages

The intimate relationship between the Alternian that is eventually spoken and written and the first language of its speakers and writers cannot be overstated, but where are those languages anyway?

Connotation SALAd

Like a lot of places that have sinister ulterior moves, the Society for Alternian Language Arts hides behind an innocent-sounding name. They're a bunch of rotten people really, what with the forced language aquicision, the "dialect cards" and discreet market manipulation techniques to ensure the underlying languages remain unexpressed.

Technically speaking, The Society for Alternian Language Arts does not allow other languages to be spoken, though this is neither expressly indicated in any part of the Society's documents nor ever enforced in any capacity (except when it would be convienient to do so) because it was only ever implied from inference of their constitution. Even with mass-hypnotism in effect, it would be impossible to influence all 1017 individuals scattered across a sphere almost 1 Mpc in radius to speak the same language, though certainly not for lack of trying.

So instead of doing their best to quash all other languages wherever they may pop up, the Society lets them be until they start causing trouble, at which point they'd use the powers their connection to the government vested to them to swiftly deal with whatever "problem" that may occur. Instead, they undermine the underlying language from the outside, by simply making it more difficult to use the underlying language in a practical measure.

Orthography

Native Script

The Native Orthography contains twenty-six separate glyphs, conveniently subdivided into seven vowels (A, E, I, J, O, U, V) and twenty-one consonants. J and V are simultaneously vowels and consonants; the orthographic vowel is a distinct concept from the phonetic one.

The letters are collated in the expected order (A, B, C, D, …, Z), but Alternian collation also includes a hidden "twenty-seventh letter" known as "End-Of-Word", which is symbolized in romanization as $ (from the regex symbol for end-of-string, $). In most Earthling collation systems, $ comes before A. The main implication is that a complete word is sorted after a partial word that starts with the same collection of letters. Deuterographies or Heterographies

The rather arcane spelling rules that are available in Alternian, though by no means less complex than that of English's, is already enough to send most Trolls into a tizzy. As such, good spelling is always a mark of high class (and lots of money to attain good education) and misspellings (heterographies) are commonplace, especially in the lower classes.

Most people however do manage to attain a more-or-less consistent spelling scheme, though that spelling scheme may have special symbols of its own or insertion or deletion of characters from the orthography. The underlying langauge, and by extension the dialectal difference between the RAL and the underlying langauge, often has a say in what letters get used in which way and how each phoneme is realized in orthography.

Below are some common heterographical characteristics:

  • Simplification of double letters: The Alternian system of double letters can be daunting even to the best spellers, and it is extremely evident in that a majority of heterographies contain some simplification of the double letter system. For vowels, instead of having dedicated letters to indicate long vowels, a "long vowel letter" can be added instead (e.g. ae → ah, ij → ih, ou → o2, o^, ~o,), or it is reduplicated (e.g. ae → aa, vu → uu or vv) or yet another symbol can be used to indicate it (e.g. ae → 4, ee → 3, ij → 1, ou → 0). Still others, due to how their Underlying Language handles vowel length, omit vowel length distinctions (e.g. ae → a, ee → e) or insert a "half-long" vowel length where there was none (e.g. a, ae → a, aa, ae).

    As for consonants, the same may also occur (e.g. pp, tt, kk being spelt as such in all positions). With consonants however, usually all the extant heterographies are just variations upon "the X form of the orthography, but extended to cover all situations." (e.g. "du", instead of being "dd"'s form in the word-final position, becomes the general form of the phoneme /dʷ/.)

  • Prepending and Appending: Sometimes Trolls "sign" their sentences by adding a unique string to the beginning, end, or both ends of a sentence. A minor case of this is visible in the orthography: ".ebba." as "!" is actually a quirk of some long-ago forgotten Troll who did jsut so happen to end sentences with the verbal tic "ebba". This string does not need to be pronounceable, nor does it need to be kept within the constraints of the twenty-six letters that are available in the orthography.
  • Total Deletions: Apart from merging some letters into other letters, there are also heterographies in which a letter is simply deleted. The offending letter may be deleted on the character level (for instance, all Js might be removed, making Tj → T but tt → tt) or the letter as a whole might be deleted (such as removing all "i"s but not "ij"s because it is a completely different vowel.) These are caused by the underlying langauge treating these letters as weakened vowels or consonants such as /ə/ or an unreleased stop and hence deleting them.
  • Replacement: For some reasons, perhaps due to bad handwriting or influence under the underlying langauge, some Trolls just can't write some letters right. Hence, there are some times when a letter is systematically, search-and-replace style transformed into another character. This can be combined with double-letter simplification above to expand the number of allowable heterographies combinatorially. Additionally, a letter can be replaced with a duplicate of itself, such as s → ss (and ss → ssss!) or du → dudu, dduu.
  • Spacing and Punctuation Changes: For some Trolls, life's too short to bother with such trivialities as prosody or paragraphs. Those Trolls tend to have heterographies that use a more-or-less scripto continua method of writing their text, including with no line breaks and no punctuation. Others write too many punctuation marks and/or tend to break up their paragraphs so much that they end up with one sentence per paragraph, every paragraph. Still others may use something other than a blank space to separate words, for instance, a "-", or a "·".

Public opinion on heterographies are relatively constant, though they do change over many hundreds of years. During this period, such heterographies are either praised as a harmless and even somewhat beneficial show of individuality, or as a dangerous perversion of the Reference Language. The two stances alternate popularity approximately once every 500 years or so, but neither one is entirely wiped out for long enough for the other to maintain permanent popularity.

This is evident when one considers the words that are used for heterographies, listed on the right, can carry slightly different connotations, but neither are considered particularly strong: "Heterography" or "Ogxo/Yuszlliw-yagtho" is generally considered the more negative of the two, due to its usage of "Yuszlliw" ("my", but more on the side of "personalized" than possession) and personalization's rather controversial position in Troll society. "Oubgxo/Wounslee-yagtho" is the positive alternative, with a "Wounslee" meaning more upon the lines of "self-selected". Of course, using "Krriggij Brrii" would obviate the whole connotation problem by simply saying "Typing Quirk" (or actually, "Quirk of Typing"), but that is not a word that upperclass Trolls would use for some reason.

Romanizations

There are two romanization schemes in use when transliterating from the Alternian Script to the Latin Alphabet:

The Common Scheme
This script directly transliterates, glyph-by-glyph, the 26 most commonly used symbols by the general population into the letters A – Z. The uncannily perfect 1:1 transliteration scheme has not gone unnoticed, but it is most appreciated because this scheme employs no diacritics of any kind and therefore is friendly to the diacritic-fearing English speaker. It however does employ some digraphs and trigraphs to match all the phonemes of the language, and furthermore has arcane respelling rules; to this end, it is more friendly to the native speaker who use those rules intuitively and apply them subconsciously than to the student who studies the language with the regular conciousness or to the linguist attempting to study the language.
The Academic Scheme
An alternate mode that guarantees 1:1 morphophonemic relationship, the Academic Scheme is so called because it is based on a spelling system that also guarantees such a relationship. It uses some diacritics to keep digraphs away, and also employs less-used letters of the Latin Alphabet such as þ (= th in the Common Scheme). Two subschemes exist: one (named "pointed", as the punctuation resemble points) faithfully duplicates the "diacritics" used in the spelling system it is based on that are actually punctuation marks (e.g. <s.> = Common sh) and the other (named "decorated", as decoration goes on top of the object) uses ordinary Latin diacritics (e.g. <š> = Common sh).

This document will use the Common Scheme, except for places where the Academic Scheme is required by context, in which case the Latin diacritics shall be employed to reduce confusion with actual punctuation. Additionally, if more than one transliteration scheme is needed, then it is displayed as the following: Common Decorated Pointed

In the year 50,502 RD, A bunch of scholars realized that the Common Scheme (that is, the Alternian Script that the Common Scheme is a direct transliteration of) is rather poor at coding Alternian well, because of its rather capricious list of respelling rules. Because of that, they have devised a new set of glyphs that has a 1:1 phonemic relationship with the RAL. However, lacking the idea that a letter can be modified by adding an extra stroke to it, they made do by combining letters with some punctuation to make the new letters that are required by the surplus of phonemes. Using the above principle, a romanization scheme can be created for human consumption.

Because of the use of punctuation as letter disambiguators, punctuation written in the academic scheme that are used as actual punctuation are surrounded by spaces, and the "punctuation as diacritic" punctuations are written with no spaces between them. Since there are only four punctuation marks in Alternian, other marks may be substituted for true punctuation marks instead (mentioned below as _ · : ?).

Punctuation

Alternian has five punctuation marks, analogous to the comma, the full-stop, the pilcrow, the apostrophe, and the question mark. They work in much the same way that their English analogs do.

The Full-Stop and Comma

The full-stop ( . : . ) is used as an idea terminator. A complete sentence may not necessarily be concluded with a full-stop; a comma ( , · , ) may be used instead. This means that what appears to be comma splices would be perfectly grammatical in Alternian. The Pilcrow

The pilcrow ( . followed by one or two newlines _ or ¶ followed by five em-spaces (     ) . followed by five em-spaces, one newline, or two newlines ) is used as an paragraph separator. Paragraphs are used much in the way that it is in English, with one paragraph holding one main idea. In addition, a lesser pilcrow ( . followed by one newline _ or ¶ followed by three em-spaces (   ) . followed by three em-spaces or one newline ) can be used to separate really long paragraphs in a way that the author desires.

The Apostrophe

Finally, the apostrophe works in much the same way as the English one does: it shortens a word or a combination of words by removing some letters. It is generally used in a handful of non-productive or idiomatic ways. Figure 4.5b shows some examples that are most commonly seen.

Unlike in English, abbreviation using apostrophes are considered appropriate in formal speech, and in some cases are considered extra-polite (especially when referring to honourable people such as the Empress or other members of royalty.) Names of people are also clipped using the apostrophe, which may be because of a name taboo (of honour or of shame alike) or simply because of convenience.

Invariably, names that are more than 6 characters long are clipped to sequences that are 6 characters long (seven, if the apostrophe is counted.) Digraphs, like "vu", "ij" or "Tj", and trigraphs like "cch", have an inconsistent definition: sometimes they are treated as one character, but others treat them as two or three characters depending on exactly how many characters are used to create the letter. The "count by letters" camp generally live in the more remote places such as Ea, Ssiounsdu, or Djekth, whereas the "count by characters" camp are both in the more central places like Wriu or extreme hinterlands like Qweewkkae or Phriutch. And just to be contrarian, those who live on the Saesson Shore tend to treat digraphs and trigraphs as two characters long.

The Name Mark

One item that is available in the native script, but not in the transliteration, is the usage of a name or noun mark. Usually, this mark is not written in the romanization, but if it is required then it is notated as the graphical approximation the semicolon (;). Otherwise, its existence is inferred by capitalization of the next available letter.

A detail about the name mark that the capitalization scheme misses is that there can be more than one name mark prefixed into the noun in question. This multiple-mark system, named Daeiouppijmee-o Na-i Fram (Dāiōṕīmē-o Na-i Fram D-ai-o-p-im-e⸗o Na⸗i Fram) is partially a test of politeness as well as an show of power – basically, the more semicolons you have, the more royal you are.

The order goes like this:

  • One semicolon is used for all nouns not covered by the bullet points below:
    • ;dwopthid
    • ;upaewd
    • ;noudf
    • ;dislaepp
    • ;oaijdd
    • ;yremss
  • Two semicolons are used for names of all people and places, with exceptions noted below:
    • ;;Ssiounsdu
    • ;;Yelfeenggin
    • ;;Friska
    • ;;Cchungyouk
    • ;;Iebblieek-yijwbth-ogch
    • ;;Tafros
  • Three semicolons are generally only used with "Alternia" and its derivatives, but several Trolls and Places have special permission to use three semicolons before their name:
    • ;;;Altrvnia – by the special provision of "Alternia"
    • ;;;Feferi – heir to the throne, Semicoloned by royal decree
    • ;;;P'aense – name of the Royal Palace, Semicoloned by royal decree
  • Four semicolons are used by all Empresses or Emperors alike…
    • ;;;;Khewp-biddli (;;;;Kh'ddli)
    • ;;;;Amsawwatt (;;;;Am'watt)
    • ;;;;Obxgrretheeu (;;;;O'theeu)
  • Except for the currently-reigning Empress, who get five semicolons. In fact, it is customary to not write the currently-reigning Empress' name itself, instead substituting it with the five semicolons.
