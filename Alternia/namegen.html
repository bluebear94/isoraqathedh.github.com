Like any other place in the universe, Alternian features have names. Unlike names outside of Alternia, given when the species still had some semblance of a child-like curiosity, names on the planet itself tend to be boring and predictable with very few surprises, the culture having matured and long since shrugged off every last scrap of morality beyond "Explore, Expand, Exploit, Exterminate". These "boring" names are usually randomly generated and overwrite older names, though your average Earthling will notice some vaguely familiar names here and there…

The Maths

Alternia's names are partially created on the concept of a random syllable. Each random Alternian word is built on the following ideas:

  • Each word is made up of one or more syllables.
    • Every word has at least one syllable.
    • If a syllable is the nth in a word, the probability that another syllable follows it is 0.64n.
    • In general, the probability that a given word has n syllables is given by the formula P(n syllables) = 0.8n(n−1)(1 − 0.64n) = 0.8n² − n − 0.8n² + n.
  • Each syllable is made up of at most one of each onset, nucleus and coda. Only the nucleus is mandatory in a syllable.
  • The rules for creating an onset is as follows:
    • If Simple Consonant Clusters are desired and there are two consonants in the coda of the preceding syllable, then instead of generating a new onset, the onset shall be "stolen" from said preceding syllable's second coda.
    • Otherwise, the following procedure is applied:
      • There is a 30% chance that the onset may be empty.
      • There is a 20% chance that the onset may consist of a single member of a P-type consonant. This may be followed by:
        • Nothing, which has a probability of 75%;
        • An F-type consonant, which has a probability of 12.5%; or
        • An R-type consonant, which has a probability of 12.5%.
        • If Simple Consonant Clusters are desired and there is one consonant in the coda of the preceding syllable, then no phonemes will be generated.
      • There is a 15% chance that the onset may consist of a single member of an L-type consonant. This may be followed by:
        • Nothing, which has a probability of 2⁄3; or
        • An R-type consonant, which has a probability of 1⁄3.
        • If Simple Consonant Clusters are desired and there is one consonant in the coda of the preceding syllable, then no phonemes will be generated.
      • There is a 15% chance that the onset may consist of a single member of an F-type consonant. This may be followed by:
        • Nothing, which has a probability of 2⁄3; or
        • An R-type consonant, which has a probability of 1⁄3.
        • If Simple Consonant Clusters are desired and there is one consonant in the coda of the preceding syllable, then no phonemes will be generated.
      • There is a 20% chance that the onset may consist of a single member of an A-type consonant. This may be followed by:
        • Nothing, which has a probability of 50%; or
        • An R-type consonant that is not also a C-type consonant, which has a probability of 50%.
        • If Simple Consonant Clusters are desired and there is one consonant in the coda of the preceding syllable, then no phonemes will be generated.
  • The rules for creating a nucleus is as follows:
    • There will always be a V-type vowel.
    • There is a 20% chance of exactly one <i> preceding said vowel.
    • There is a 20% chance of exactly one <w> succeding said vowel.
    • <yi>, <yii>, <uw>, <uuw>, and any sequence of <yVw> where V is the aforementioned V-type vowel are not valid nuclei.
  • The rules for creating a coda is as follows:
    • There is a 50% chance that the coda may be empty.
    • There is a 30% chance that the coda may be a single P-type consonant that is not <h>. This may be followed by:
      • Nothing, which has a probability of 2⁄3; or
      • An F-type consonant, which has a probability of 1⁄3.
    • There is a 10% chance that the coda may be a single L-type consonant.
    • There is a 10% chance that the coda may be a single F-type consonant.
V-type vowel
Any one of <a, e, i, o, u, ae, ee, ij, ou, vu>. They are pronounced [ɑ, ɛ, i, ɔ, ʊ, ɑː, ɛː, iː, ɔː, ʊː] respectively.
A-type consonant
Any one of <y, w>. They are pronounced [j, w] respectively.
P-type consonant
Any one of <p, t, k, b, d, g, h, m, n>. They are pronounced [p, t, k, b, d, g, h, m, n] respectively.
L-type consonant
Any one of <pp, tt, kk, bb, dd, gg>. They are pronounced [pʰ, tʰ, kʰ, bʷ, dʷ, gʷ] respectively.
F-type consonant
Any one of <th, f, s, x, ss>. They are pronounced [θ, f, s, x, ʃ] respectively.
R-type consonant
Any one of <rl, l, r, rr>. They are pronounced [ɾ, l, ɹ, r] respectively.
C-type consonant
Any A-, P-, L-, F-, or R-type consonant that isn't <r, rr>.

The string is then changed so the aesthetics of each word looks slightly better. The changes are all based on these rules. These rules are executed in the following order:

  1. Unless contradicted by the following rules, all letters use its G form.
  2. Word-initially, L-type consonants use their J forms.
  3. At the end of words, L- and F-type consonants use their H forms.
  4. If an F-type consonant finds itself succeeding exactly one other phoneme in a syllable, it uses its I form.
  5. If an L-, F-, or R-type leter finds itself in such a way that when taken with the letter after it the two form a pair of doubled characters, then the former will change into its K form.
  6. If a V-type character finds itself in such a way that when taken with the letter after it the two form a pair of doubled characters, then the one latter will change into its K form.
  7. <ss> is spelt <j> when preceded with <d>.
  8. <tss> is spelt <cch>.
  9. <hl>, <hr>, <hll> and <hrr> are sometimes but not always spelt <l>, <r>, <ll> and <rr> repsectively.
  10. Any character that is repeated more than three times after these transformations are reduced into repeating only twice.
  11. Some customary names use the I form for L-type consonants.

To remember the names, notice that:

  • The G form is used when you're laying the groundwork of the word;
  • The H form is used when you're happy (done) with the word;
  • The I form is primarily used in important people's names;
  • The J form is used to jump-start the word; and
  • The K form stops doubled characters from knocking on each other.
