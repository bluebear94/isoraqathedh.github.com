Basic Word Order

Word order is dependent on the gender of the object, if present.

If the verb has valence 1:

  • Object markers are not used; verb markers are.
  • Every such verb is either object-biased or subject-biased.
    • If it is object-biased, then
      • If the gender of the word is ANIMATE FEMININE or INANIMATE MASCULINE, the order is VO
      • Else, it is OV
    • Otherwise, it is a subject-biased verb, and
      • If it is ANIMATE FEMININE or INANIMATE masculine, the order is SV
      • Else, it is VS
    • Gender otherwise has no impact on the general inflectional morphology of a word.

Below are examples of various verbs with different biases and valences. Notice how lot of words that are valence-3 and valence-0 verbs in English become valence-1 object-biased words.

Valence-2 words are OSV, except when:

  • Both object and subject are inanimate,
  • It is followed by an interconnected valence-1 sentence, or
  • It is a verb where the object and the subject are interchangeable, as in the case of "choose".

Those words use OVS, or NVN in the case of "choose", because the ordering of the choice does not matter.

Gender

In Alternian, all verbs have gender, but only a handful of nouns do, and verbs have a different gender system than adjectives and adverbs. Verbs can be: Inanimate Feminine, Animate Masculine, Inanimate Masculine, and Animate Feminine; while adjectives and adverbs can be: Inanimate Unnatural, Animate Natural, Inanimate Natural, and Animate Unnatural.

In any case, what is masculine and what is feminine is completely arbitrary – there are no real criteria for either one and they could just as appropriately named "Blop" and "Flibbertygibbet" – but the other two sub-genders have fairly rigourous definitions.

A verb is animate if it is commonly done by living things or can only can be done by living things. Verbs like "think", "taste", "react", "steal" and "cough" are animate. Otherwise, they are inanimate. Examples of this type include "bounce", "form", "fall", "detach" and "attach". Notice that actions done by cadavers are considered animate, such as "decompose", "rot", and "be scavenged".

Unnatural adjectives are adjectives that describe things not commonly found in the natural world. For instance, "pink" is an unnatural adjective, because pink items are not commonly found in nature (pink leaves do exist but are limited to the hottest parts of Alternia). Other unnatural adjectives include "square", "black", "patterned/checkerboard/gidded" and "artificial" (well, duh). Natural adjectives include "orange", "mottled", "rough", "flowy" and "flexible".

As noted earlier, nouns are genderless, but there are exceptions, such as:

  • Animals, especially those that are domesticated or tamed. Oddly enough, the gender-specific versions of the animal names may not even match with the genders of the animal in question. Mares are female, yet "mare" is masculine!
  • Several toponyms near the poles also have a gender due to the underlying language. It's most pronounced in Ea, though they crop up in other places sporadically.
  • Sets of tools also get genders, for example the pick/axe/shovel set, the spoon/fork/knife/skewer/ladle/chopper set, and the pen/pencil/rubber/ruler/compasses set. Again, the internal divisions can be very arbitrary.
  • Emphatically not names, Troll or otherwise.

In ordinary discourse, no genders are marked for anything, but in higher level discourse, they manifest as euphonic changes in the last vowel.

Word Construction

Several grammatical forms are seen in Lower speechthat are not seen in upper speech. Most notably, where Lower speech uses these structures in order to describe new concepts, Upper speech depends on new root words in order to label those exact same subjects. These forms can be described by the following EBNF: 

Form        = base object, that, any clause, [but not, any clause] 
            | clause, (" " | "" | "-"), base object;
Any clause  = clause | and clauses | or clauses;
Or clauses  = or is also, clause, clause, {clause}, end of or;
And clauses = and can also, clause, clause, {clause}, end of and;
Clause      = (specializes on | possesses | looks like), [part, part of], metaphor object;

In this case, words in italics represent non-terminals and ordinary type represent direct glosses of Alternian words or parts of words, which are translated below. Notice that though in theory or- and and-clauses can support any number of clauses, in practice no more than 4 and usually only two are used. In translating these terms, comparatively simpler terms can use more idiomatic ways of translating to the target language, e.g. "hoof-beast" (horse/equinoid) instead of "beast that possesses hoof".
