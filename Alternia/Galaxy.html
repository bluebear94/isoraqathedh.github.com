Astronomically, Alternia is not a particularly interesting planet. It lives in what can charitably be called a metropolis in a disconnected and isolated community – the Alternian Galaxy. The galaxy itself is sequestered in one of the scariest, emptiest places in the universe, with nary a plume of gas in many parsecs around.

The Alternian Universe is even more bog-standard. The universe as a whole is quite homogenous, with thin strips of galaxy clusters spanning and surrounding very large voids, which contains very little. This layout is very much like ours, which may or may not be a coincidence.


The Alternian Galaxy

The Alternian Galaxy is the home, and so far only, Galaxy that hosts the Trolls. It is a void galaxy, meaning that it exists in an environment that's relatively empty of galaxies. This has vast implications with the society.

Details

The galaxy, as will be noted, is very old, as is the universe. Being approximately fifty-seven billion years into its existence, the universe formed this galaxy about four billion years after its birth, and hence the age of the galaxy is 53 billion years old.

The galaxy contains roughly 40% more stars than the Milky Way, but on average each star only hosts 80% of the mass of an average star in the Milky Way, so the galactic mass isn't very much bigger than ours. Mainly K and M stars dominate.

For a distant observer not moving relative to the galaxy, the galaxy has a distinctive red glow common for aging galaxies, from all the red stars both young (red giants that are about eight to to twelve billion years old) and old (small red dwarves with lifetimes over one trillion years). The main implications of this is that the stars that are in it are already run down, and any new life that may occur would at best be isolative and resigned and at worst be non-existent.

The Location of Alternia

The Alternian System is within the Alternian Galaxy (surprisingly enough). Currently, the star is about 40% the way in towards the middle of the aging and collapsing spiral galaxy, making one circuit around the galactic centre in about 150 Myr.

The system is not in any of the decaying spiral arms; it is now stuck in the now growing elliptical core which is growing at a rate of about five to fifty stars every year. The central black hole has the size of about 1.6 × 107 Msol. Its mass is growing only on the order of tonnes a year, and very rarely capture stars or stellar remnants, usually only sucking in what's left of the stellar gas.

Elsewhere in the Galaxy

Due to large amounts of geoengineering done by the Trolls, most planets are either uninhabitable to Trolls or have been massively terraformed. There is also an undisputed monopoly of power at the hands of the Trolls, though they themselves squabble quite a bit.

As such there are very little, if any, life that isn't already a subject to the Trolls. Exceptions include "desert planets", planets that cannot support water in its liquid form (this includes gas giants and rogue planets); "waterlogged planets", planets that can support water but there's too much of it (generally, a planet's a "lost cause" if it has no oceans less than 42 km deep); and the oft-neglected "nail planets", planets that have put up so much of a fight that to fight them is an untenable liability. This third class of planets are barely tolerated, and are often glossed over in the official textbooks. Unconquered life also exists in conquered planets, often in the form of subterranean miners and deep-water-dwellers, environments that are hostile to Trolls.

Beyond the Galaxy

To the Trolls, there is no other "elsewhere" – the void filament that the Alternian Galaxy resides in is simply too thin, the next galaxy too far away. While these galaxies are by no means undetectable, they are by severely distant – the nearest galaxy, called Benskans, is 35.1 ± 0.044 Mly from Alternia, which, in other words, is quite far away.

However, with our privileged viewpoint, we can look further into the void and out into the more lively sections of the universe at large.

The Alternian Void

The Alternian Void is a fairly large void, with a diameter of about 110 Mpc. Being a void, it contains very few galaxies, and the gas around here is very very sparse, with one lone hydrogen atom floating around every 105 ~ 106 cm3, and heavier atoms seen in even smaller amounts. These tiny atoms zip around, almost never colliding with each other.

The view around here is equally depressing: a dark expanse, the light from the distant filaments all but faded into invisibility. Though the gas is sparse, it's still there, and the Void is so large that what little light that could have reached your eyes would still be scattered away. With only your eyes, you can't see anything. Poetically speaking, it would be the closest thing to oblivion you can ever see.

Objects in the Void

How do you know all this?

Though the Trolls have no records of these rogue planets, their behaviour and fate can be predicted both from rogue planets of our own universe's, and of galaxy-orbiting rogue planets which are documented in Alternia.

Obviously, the Void is just empty, not completely empty. Every thousand parsecs or so, you will encounter a rogue planet. These planets are smoother than regular planets, because they (a) don't have that many things to hit them with and (b) things that do hit them tend to wear them down more than they make big holes, because the the offending collider is often a small chip of rock, which makes no bigger holes than they need to snugly fit into.

The age of the universe means that a majority of rogue planets already had their internal heat supply, given either by radioactive decay of trace amounts of superheavy metals or by any satellites (very, very rare indeed) adding tidal stress to the planet, almost exhausted. There are no records on individual rogue planets – remember, these things are far away, tiny, and emit pretty much no light at all, as well as pointless and boring – but these are their general properties.

Void filaments make their strenuous way across the void as well, metaphorically piercing half-heartedly poking the darkness with their dim, ~700 nm light. The Alternian Galaxy live in such a void filament, along with almost every other galaxy that lives in the Void. The closest void filament from the Alternian Filament is almost 20 Mpc from Alternia, almost twice as far as the nearest galaxy from Alternia, Benskans, which as indicated above is 10.76 Mpc away.

Are there any galaxies in the void that isn't part of a void filament? Yes. It's a little irregular galaxy, 31 Mpc away. It lies in almost the opposite direction of Benskans to Alternia, and shows up as a blurry magnitude-15 spot in Alternian telescopes. The filament turns slightly at Alternia so it doesn't actually contain this little galaxy, which is, while known, still not named, as it is generally accepted that it is pointless to know about this stupid thing anyway.

The Four Bounding Filaments

Note: these items are located using the equatorial coordinate system, with the centre set at the centre of Alternia in the present day. For orthographically convenient reasons however, Celestial Longitude, which is measured in degrees, replaces right ascension, which is measured in hours. 0° Celestial Longitude is equivalent to 0h right ascension. Declination is similarly changed to use °N and °S in place of + and − respectively.

There are four filaments that define the boundaries of the void, and they are relatively stringy, very aged, and of course unnamed due to general Troll ignorance about them, be it true or enforced. These filaments span a good 50° or so of the Alternian sky, but are usually no thicker than the galaxies that they contain. Their lengths, from shortest to longest, are 150 ± 25, 200 ± 25, 375 ± 25, and 425 ± 25 Mpc long. As they lack a convenient name to refer to, this article with refer to them using their lengths, as "One Hundred", "Two Hundred", "Three Hundred" and "Four Hundred".

The nearest of these four filaments is Two Hundred, which is also the one that comes closest to the Celestial South Pole. Containing about one galaxy cluster every 50 to 70 Mpc at its thickest and every 400 to 600 Mpc at its thinnest. Two Hundred primarily contains old galaxies and looks yellowish when viewed from a sufficient distance. At its closest point, 58°S 24°E, it is about 43 Mpc from Alternia.

The next closest one, One Hundred, is slightly younger than Two Hundred, but it's also quite small. This one stretches a diagonal line across the Celestial Sphere, starting from 30°S 141°E, coming to its closest point at 6°N 81°E, and fading back out at 48°N 19°E. It's about 64 Mpc from Alternia, and is at that point magnitude 25 (although the non-negligible redshift at these distance may render this number rather useless.)

Three Hundred is the next closest one after One Hundred, and is about 80 Mpc away from Alternia at its closest. This filament is the brightest and densest one of them them all, but it doesn't contain as much mass as Four Hundred. On average, there is a supercluster every 50 to 60 Mpc, which is significantly more than that of Two Hundred. On the Celestial Sphere, it starts at 50°N 15°W and ends at 0°N 121°W, with its maximal brightness of magnitude 26.4 achieved at 22°N 70°W.

The most distant and most massive Four Hundred is on the far end of the thick end of the Alternian Void. It's almost 120 Mpc away from Alternia at its closest point, and spans a rough line from 80°N 128°W to 40°N 45°E, coming closest towards the Celestial North Pole at about 88°N 64°W. Four Hundred is also younger than the other filaments by a margin of several billion years, and if you were close enough to see it, it glows bright white, in contrast of the dull orange-yellow glow of nearby filaments.

Nearby Superstructures

The closest non-Void supercluster to Alternia resides in One Hundred. This supercluster, which doesn't even have a number to itself, contains about 2,200 galaxies, grouped into about 400 clusters. Like all superclusters, this one is slowly drifting apart because gravity is too weak to hold it together. .

(Incidentally, this is also the nearest supercluster full stop to Alternia, because there are no superclusters in the Alternian Void, in the sense that there are no gravitationally unbound but proximate groups of gravitationally bound galaxy clusters. Individual clusters, or even individual galaxies, are strung together to form the void filaments, and no superclusters are involved.)

Beyond the Four Boundary Filaments, there exist a few other other filaments within 500 Mpc (z = 0.01). The longest of these is around 540 ± 30 Mpc long and 70: kpc wide and thick, and continues more or less perpendicularly to One Hundred. At its closest, it is 440 ± 15 Mpc away from Alternia, and this point is roughly at coordinates 32°N 174°W. The largest supercluster in 500 Mpc is in this filament, and contains about 6,000 galaxies broken up into about 500 groups. Light from this supercluster, and this filament is orange, given proximity.

Walls, also known as fat filaments, do not exist within 500 Mpc of Alternia, though two exist within 1 Gpc. The first one, with point of closest approach at about 700 Mpc, is 3 Mpc thick, 33 Mpc wide and 400 Mpc long. The other one has a closest approach of around 900 Mpc, and is 9 Mpc thick, 50 Mpc wide and so long that one end falls beyond magnitude 40 – an estimation is that it's almost 1 Gpc long.

Unusually, there's also a fat wall, or a full block of galaxies, within 1 Gpc of Alternia. This "block" as a whole is magnitude 35, and is 300 Mpc tall, 200 Mpc wide and 250 Mpc thick, making it undeniably not a filament or a wall. In the celestial sphere, its coordinates are 18°S, 33°W. There is no consensus of where this block came from, mainly because no Troll has heard (and will ever hear) about it.

The Large-Scale Structure

The large-scale structure of the Alternian universe is believed to be much the same as the large-scale structure of our universe: long, stringy filaments of clusters and superclusters surrounding huge voids of nearly nothing. The overall density of voids and filaments are roughly the same throughout the universe, making them look like a Petri dish of soap bubbles.

There are differences though. One that stands out almost immediately is that the Alternian Universe is much larger – relative to Alternia, the radius of the observable universe is around 450 Gly, consistent with its 57 Ga age. The Alternian universe is also growing much faster than our universe, with its Hubble constant currently evaluated to be about 135.43 ± 2.4 km s−1 Mpc.

There are no doubt countless interesting features in the Alternian universe, and a vast majority of them are not aware that some planet nine billion kersplodges away has been completely obliterated. One may even think that the universe as a whole emerged quite unscathed from the whole 0 RD problem – it continues to age, and teeter towards its eventual final end state.

There may even be life in these distant domains, so far away that they are out of reach of the Trolls. Some of them might even have managed to catch wind of this difficult species and prayed that they never reach them, or scoff at their technological and/or moral inexpertise as they attempt to slaughter and enslave all they encounter in an attempt to rule over their little patch of land. There may even be intelligent species that will never hear of the Trolls. In any case, it may be best for those ignorant people to never hear of the Trolls and vice versa, for who can predict the explosive consequences that will ensue?

Beyond the Universe

The limit of the observable universe from Alternia is 450 Gly or about 140 Gpc, which you may compare to ours' 93 Gly. The actual Alternian universe is a bit bigger than that, but we truly do not know how large it is. We do know however it has an edge of some description – if not in the three spatial dimensions then at least at one end of the time dimension, and for certain it is bounded in one of the non-classical spatial dimensions – because things have been referred as to be "outside" this Universe.

What lies in this strange land? The interuniversal medium is a strange place to encounter. Because we lie beyond the boundary of this universe, linear distances that span beyond this boundary become mere mathematical abstractions, with very little bearing on what the physical world holds. Rather than transcending all time and space however, the interuniversal medium merely resides in its own time and space, although in this case time and space are even more similar than usual due to the strange and wonderful fact that time in the interuniversal medium is directed, but not wholly unidirectional.

What does the boundary look like? Well, the problem with most universes is that you can't usually escape them – they act a little bit like a 3-torus – an Asteroids gamefield in three dimensions – but it looks like a sphere from the inside, in which you are returned to the antipodal point. To escape the universe, you need to move in a completely different direction to these directions.

But once you do, you will be greeted with the view that is wonderfully encapsulated in Fig. 2.1a. You will have to acquaint yourself with the new coordinate system. Things are very foggy in there, and since this is a different time and space, you may move three inches and find that you have left the Alternian universe far behind already.

What this place has to offer are universes and more universes, those that are of utter uselessness to any form of life and others that proliferate with (and sometimes, plagued with) them. These things are not fixed relative to the space-time that this strange interuniversal medium has. Indeed, if it wasn't for this being a mental exercise, it is not known if you'll even pass the barrier and still exist on the other side.

If you manage to not just exist there but to survive there however, you can move. And once you move far enough, you might just find Earth.

But that's a story for another time.
